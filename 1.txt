id="b59b" data-selectable-paragraph="">Let's start with,</p><h2 data-omnivore-anchor-idx="37" data-selectable-paragraph="" id="09db">What is Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG)?</h2><p data-omnivore-anchor-idx="38" id="14a9" data-selectable-paragraph="">First, let’s briefly recap RAG. Traditionally, Large Language Models (LLMs) generate responses solely based on the data they were trained on. This can lead to issues like:</p><blockquote data-omnivore-anchor-idx="39"><p data-omnivore-anchor-idx="40" id="a952" data-selectable-paragraph=""><strong data-omnivore-anchor-idx="41">Hallucinations:</strong> Generating factually incorrect or nonsensical information.</p><p data-omnivore-anchor-idx="42" id="e86b" data-selectable-paragraph=""><strong data-omnivore-anchor-idx="43">Outdated information:</strong> Lacking knowledge of recent events or developments after their training cutoff.</p><p data-omnivore-anchor-idx="44" id="2ba0" data-selectable-paragraph=""><strong data-omnivore-anchor-idx="45">Lack of domain-specific knowledge:</strong> Unable to answer questions about proprietary or niche data.</p></blockquote><p data-omnivore-anchor-idx="46" id="e45e" data-selectable-paragraph="">RAG addresses these limitations by augmenting the LLM’s knowledge with external, up-to-date, and domain-specific information. The general RAG workflow typically involves:</p><ol data-omnivore-anchor-idx="47"><li data-omnivore-anchor-idx="48" id="3f8f" data-selectable-paragraph=""><strong data-omnivore-anchor-idx="49">Retrieval:</strong> When a user asks a question, a retrieval mechanism (often using embeddings and vector databases) searches a vast knowledge base (documents, articles, web pages, etc.) for relevant information.</li><li data-omnivore-anchor-idx="50" id="efba" data-selectable-paragraph=""><strong data-omnivore-anchor-idx="51">Augmentation:</strong> The retrieved information is then provided to the LLM as additional context alongside the user’s original query.</li><li data-omnivore-anchor-idx="52" id="4f44" data-selectable-paragraph=""><strong data-omnivore-anchor-idx="53">Generation:</strong> The LLM uses this augmented prompt to generate a more informed, accurate, and contextually relevant response.</li></ol><h2 data-omnivore-anchor-idx="54" data-selectable-paragraph="" id="15a6"><strong data-omnivore-anchor-idx="55"><em data-omnivore-anchor-idx="56">What is Structured RAG, Really? It’s Logic vs. Vibes</em></strong></h2><p data-omnivore-anchor-idx="57" id="5b68" data-selectable-paragraph="">Let me give you a full picture in detail.</p><figure data-omnivore-anchor-idx="58"><div data-selectable-paragraph="">The AI industry loves its jargon, so let’s cut through it. The difference between Normal RAG and Structured RAG is a fundamental shift in philosophy.</p><p data-omnivore-anchor-idx="66" id="3c2c" data-selectable-paragraph=""><strong data-omnivore-anchor-idx="67">Normal RAG operates on “Vibes” (Semantic Similarity).</strong> It’s like asking a very knowledgeable, enthusiastic friend who has speed-read every book on a topic. It uses vector embeddings to find text chunks that have a similar <em data-omnivore-anchor-idx="68">geometric</em> position in a high-dimensional space. It understands the “gist” and is brilliant for exploratory questions. But its reliance on proximity means it can easily confuse nuance for noise.</p><p data-omnivore-anchor-idx="69" id="4adc" data-selectable-paragraph=""><strong data-omnivore-anchor-idx="70">Structured RAG operates on “Logic” (Symbolic Representation).</strong> It’s like putting a legal expert on the witness stand inside a library where every book has been meticulously cross-referenced. This expert doesn’t care about “semantic feel.” They operate on a knowledge graph built from facts, entities, actions, and the explicit relationships between them.
